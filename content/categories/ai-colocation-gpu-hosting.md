# AI Colocation / GPU Hosting Partners

This category covers providers offering colocation, GPU hosting, managed capacity, or related deployment options relevant to AI infrastructure buyers. These vendors matter when teams need capacity faster than a build can deliver, want to avoid full facility ownership, or prefer a hybrid deployment strategy.

For many buyers, hosted capacity is not a fallback. It is the fastest practical path to getting workloads live while preserving optionality around future modular builds, owned facilities, or phased infrastructure control.

## Why this category matters

- colocation and GPU hosting can close the time gap between demand and owned infrastructure readiness
- hosted options may reduce capital intensity and execution risk compared with immediate greenfield deployment
- buyers need to compare not just available racks or GPUs, but power density, operational model, and strategic flexibility
- this category often sits at the decision fork between rent, partner, build, or mix approaches over time

## Where it fits in a modular deployment

- acts as a fast-capacity or bridge strategy while owned modular infrastructure is still being planned or built
- may remain part of the long-term architecture in hybrid or multi-site operating models
- interacts with network, interconnection, workload placement, and colocation ecosystem considerations
- is often compared directly against modular builds, retrofits, or bare-metal hosting options

## What to compare

- geographic coverage and proximity to target customers, talent, or connectivity hubs
- density readiness, power envelope, and cooling capability for AI workloads
- ecosystem and interconnection value around carriers, cloud on-ramps, and service partners
- deployment speed, contract flexibility, and expansion path
- suitability for training, inference, or mixed workload profiles

## Typical buyer questions

- Is hosted capacity a bridge, a hedge, or the long-term answer for this workload?
- How much density and operational flexibility does the provider actually support?
- What are we gaining in speed, and what are we giving up in control?
- How well does this location fit network, talent, and customer-adjacency needs?
- Could a hybrid model outperform a pure hosted or pure owned strategy?

## Decision scenarios

AI colocation and GPU hosting decisions should be treated as strategy choices around speed, control, network position, and capital exposure. The right provider can bridge capacity while owned infrastructure matures, or become a durable part of a hybrid deployment model.

- Urgent training or inference launch: compare available density, GPU readiness, contract terms, network access, and expansion options before committing to owned build timelines.
- Hybrid owned-plus-hosted roadmap: evaluate which workloads belong in hosted environments, which justify modular ownership, and how network fabric connects the two.
- Geographic demand placement: prioritize latency, customer proximity, data-sovereignty constraints, cloud/on-ramp access, and regional power/cooling maturity.
- Capital-light scaling: assess whether contract flexibility, service model, and upgrade path offset reduced control over facility decisions.

## Deployment signals

- Demand is live before owned capacity, permits, utility work, or modular delivery can catch up.
- The buyer needs AI-ready density but does not yet want full facility ownership risk.
- Network, location, ecosystem, or cloud adjacency matters as much as raw rack availability.
- Hosted capacity is becoming part of a portfolio strategy rather than a temporary fallback.
