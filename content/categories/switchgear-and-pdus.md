# Switchgear, PDUs, and Busway

Switchgear, PDUs, busway, and related distribution gear form the core electrical delivery layer between utility or backup power sources and high-density compute loads. This category covers the equipment that makes capacity usable, segmentable, and maintainable inside real facilities.

For many deployments, this is where abstract megawatt planning becomes rack-level reality. Distribution architecture influences uptime, maintenance risk, expandability, and how gracefully a site can absorb future density increases.

## Why this category matters

- distribution design determines how power is segmented, protected, and delivered across the site
- poor switchgear or PDU planning creates maintenance headaches, bottlenecks, and resiliency risk later
- modular and phased deployments need distribution layers that can expand without forcing major rework
- buyers often need to compare standardization, serviceability, lead times, and integration with upstream power design

## Where it fits in a modular deployment

- downstream of utility service, generators, and UPS systems
- upstream of racks, cabinet power, cooling plant auxiliaries, and facility controls
- tightly linked to layout, module design, redundancy model, and maintenance strategy
- often becomes one of the most practical decision points in prefab and staged expansion models

## What to compare

- redundancy support and maintenance isolation capability
- density readiness, breaker strategy, and distribution flexibility
- busway versus cable-heavy distribution tradeoffs
- lead times, footprint efficiency, and service accessibility
- fit for modular skids, packaged power rooms, or retrofit environments

## Typical buyer questions

- Can the distribution layer support present density and future growth without major redesign?
- How easy is it to isolate faults or perform maintenance without affecting live loads?
- What does the equipment footprint look like inside a constrained module or white space?
- Are lead times acceptable relative to the rest of the project schedule?
- How much coordination is required with UPS, controls, and rack-enclosure choices?

## Decision scenarios

Distribution decisions become strategic when buyers need to keep capacity expandable without making every future phase a major outage or redesign. This category should be researched with upstream power, UPS, and rack-density assumptions close at hand.

- Phased modular campus: compare busway, switchboards, packaged power rooms, and standardized PDU blocks so each future module can connect without reworking live sections.
- High-density retrofit: evaluate panel capacity, breaker strategy, fault isolation, metering, and space constraints before adding new GPU racks or liquid-cooling auxiliaries.
- Lead-time constrained build: identify whether standard gear, pre-engineered skids, alternate vendors, or staged distribution can protect the deployment schedule.
- Maintenance-sensitive operation: prioritize architectures that support isolation, safe access, monitoring, and replacement workflows without broad load exposure.

## Deployment signals

- Future rack density is uncertain, but the buyer wants distribution choices that will not box in expansion.
- The site has power available upstream, yet floor-level distribution is becoming the practical bottleneck.
- Equipment lead times are threatening the build schedule more than civil or shell work.
- Operations teams are pushing for clearer metering, maintainability, and fault segmentation before accepting the design.
