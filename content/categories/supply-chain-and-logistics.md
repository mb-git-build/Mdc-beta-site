# Supply Chain and Logistics

Supply chain and logistics categories cover manufacturing lead times, transport planning, staging, customs or cross-border movement, delivery sequencing, craning, and the practical movement of modular infrastructure into place.

This category matters because modular delivery only looks simple from far away. In real projects, logistics can quietly control schedule, cost, install risk, and whether factory-built advantages actually survive contact with the site.

## Why this category matters

- transport and staging constraints can erase the speed advantage of modular delivery if not handled early
- long-lead equipment, route planning, and sequencing affect when usable capacity can come online
- buyers need to understand not only manufacturing timelines but the physical path from factory to site
- logistics strategy becomes more complex when projects span regions, constrained roads, or phased deployments

## Where it fits in a modular deployment

- bridges factory-built systems with site execution reality
- interacts directly with modular packaging choices, site access, crane planning, and installation sequencing
- affects when electrical, cooling, and enclosure components can actually be commissioned
- becomes especially important for large modules, remote sites, international sourcing, and compressed schedules

## What to compare

- manufacturing lead times versus real delivered-on-site timing
- transport constraints, route complexity, and staging requirements
- experience handling oversized, sensitive, or high-value infrastructure components
- coordination strength across factory, carrier, site, and commissioning teams
- resilience to schedule changes, phased deliveries, and unexpected site conditions

## Typical buyer questions

- What is the real end-to-end timeline from order to install-ready delivery?
- Are there route, permit, or crane constraints that could break the schedule?
- Which components are most exposed to long-lead or cross-border risk?
- How much schedule confidence comes from the logistics plan versus optimistic assumptions?
- Can the delivery model support phased rollout without chaos on-site?
