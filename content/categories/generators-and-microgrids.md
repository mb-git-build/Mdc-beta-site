# Generators and Microgrids

Generator and microgrid categories cover on-site power resilience, islanding strategies, backup generation, fuel planning, and the broader energy architecture around AI-ready data center growth.

This category matters whenever utility timelines, power quality, resiliency goals, or energy strategy push a project beyond simple grid dependence. For many modular deployments, generation strategy is part of the business case, not just a disaster-recovery afterthought.

## Why this category matters

- generation and microgrid design can determine whether capacity comes online at all while utility constraints persist
- resilience strategy affects uptime, fuel logistics, environmental constraints, and long-term operating economics
- buyers increasingly need to compare backup-only models against more active site-energy strategies
- the generator layer is tightly connected to UPS, switchgear, interconnection timing, and site selection decisions

## Where it fits in a modular deployment

- upstream of critical loads as part of resilience and continuity architecture
- often paired with modular deployments where utility upgrades lag project demand
- may support temporary, phased, or hybrid operating models while long-term capacity matures
- becomes especially relevant in remote sites, constrained grids, and fast-growth AI environments

## What to compare

- standby versus prime-power role and expected operating profile
- fuel strategy, maintenance burden, and environmental/permitting constraints
- microgrid controls, islanding capability, and flexibility under changing load conditions
- integration with UPS, switchgear, battery systems, and energy management layers
- vendor ability to support commissioning, field service, and long-term operational reliability

## Typical buyer questions

- Is generation only for backup, or part of the actual capacity plan?
- How exposed is the project to fuel availability, emissions rules, or noise constraints?
- Can this architecture bridge delayed utility capacity without creating long-term problems?
- What role should batteries or microgrid controls play alongside generators?
- How much operational complexity is the team taking on in exchange for speed or resilience?

## Decision scenarios

Generator and microgrid decisions become strategic when the buyer is using on-site power to bridge utility delays, protect uptime, or support a broader energy model. The category should be researched with permitting, fuel logistics, UPS, switchgear, and site strategy in the same conversation.

- Utility-delay bridge: compare temporary generation, phased utility upgrades, emissions limits, fuel logistics, and switchgear integration before committing to a fast-launch modular plan.
- Resilience-first AI site: evaluate standby capacity, N+1 assumptions, battery ride-through, microgrid controls, testing cadence, and maintenance access as one operating model.
- Remote or weak-grid deployment: prioritize fuel supply, islanding controls, service response, load-following behavior, and environmental constraints before selecting equipment.
- Hybrid energy strategy: assess how generators interact with batteries, renewables, demand response, reporting, and future utility interconnection rather than treating backup power as isolated.

## Deployment signals

- The business wants capacity online before the utility can deliver permanent service.
- Fuel, emissions, or acoustic constraints are now shaping site feasibility.
- Backup generation is drifting into prime or bridge-power territory and needs a different operating plan.
- UPS, switchgear, and controls teams cannot finalize architecture until generation behavior is defined.
