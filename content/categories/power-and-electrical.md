# Power and Electrical Infrastructure

Power and electrical infrastructure is the backbone of any serious modular or conventional data center deployment. This category spans the upstream electrical chain that turns utility access or on-site generation into usable, resilient capacity for racks, cooling systems, controls, and critical operations.

In practice, power is often the real constraint behind growth plans. Teams may have land, capital, demand, and compute strategy, but expansion still stalls if the electrical architecture cannot support density targets, redundancy requirements, or speed-to-deployment goals.

## Why this category matters

- power availability usually determines whether a modular project is even feasible on the desired timeline
- electrical architecture affects redundancy, fault tolerance, maintenance windows, and scaling flexibility
- power-layer decisions shape the design of UPS, switchgear, busway, generator, and cooling strategies downstream
- buyers often need vendors that can work across greenfield builds, phased rollouts, retrofits, and temporary capacity expansions

## Where it fits in a modular deployment

- upstream of switchgear, UPS, PDUs, and busway distribution layers
- tightly linked to generator and microgrid strategy when utility capacity is constrained
- directly impacts cooling design because higher-density environments increase both electrical and thermal requirements
- becomes central early in planning because site selection, interconnection timing, and modular design all depend on the power envelope

## What to compare

- available power capacity, scalability path, and high-density readiness
- integration with modular, prefab, retrofit, or staged deployment models
- resilience design, failover logic, and maintainability under live operations
- delivery speed, commissioning capability, and field support depth
- track record in mission-critical or data-center-grade environments

## Typical buyer questions

- How much usable capacity can this architecture support today versus after expansion?
- What redundancy model is realistic for the target budget and deployment speed?
- Can the vendor support modular phases, temporary capacity, or hybrid utility plus generation models?
- What commissioning and operations burden will this create after handoff?
- Where are the biggest risks, utility delay, equipment lead time, or integration complexity?

## Decision scenarios

Power decisions usually become urgent when the site plan, density target, and utility timeline stop lining up. Use these scenarios to decide which adjacent categories to research next.

- Utility-constrained greenfield site: compare site selection, interconnection timing, generators, microgrids, staged modular blocks, and utility-facing field execution before choosing a final electrical topology.
- High-density AI retrofit: evaluate switchgear, busway, UPS, rack density, thermal rejection, relay/protection impacts, and acceptance-testing burden together so the power upgrade does not strand cooling capacity.
- Temporary or phased capacity expansion: prioritize vendors that can support packaged electrical rooms, modular skids, commissioning, energization sequencing, and monitoring during live operations.
- Resilience-first deployment: map redundancy, maintenance bypass, generator runtime, battery autonomy, monitoring, protection testing, and incident response as one operating model.

## Deployment signals

- The project has demand and land, but utility capacity or interconnection schedule is now the gating constraint.
- Rack density assumptions changed after GPU selection, forcing upstream electrical redesign.
- A phased build needs temporary power, packaged distribution, or modular electrical rooms before the permanent plant is ready.
- Operations teams need clearer monitoring and failover visibility before accepting the commissioned site.
