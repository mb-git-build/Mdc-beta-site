# Modular / Prefabricated Data Center Vendors

This category covers vendors focused on modular, prefabricated, packaged, or factory-integrated data center infrastructure. It is the anchor category for deployments where speed, repeatability, phased growth, or constrained sites make traditional construction less attractive.

Modular is not just a facility format. It is often a delivery model that touches power, cooling, controls, integration, commissioning, logistics, and long-term operational flexibility. That is why this category sits near the center of the broader market graph.

## Why this category matters

- modular can compress delivery timelines and reduce on-site construction complexity
- packaged systems can make phased growth easier when demand is uncertain or power arrives in stages
- the modular model often changes vendor coordination, factory acceptance, transport planning, and site-prep strategy
- buyers need to compare more than the shell, they need to understand what is actually integrated and what remains site-built

## Where it fits in a modular deployment

- serves as the central packaged layer combining electrical, cooling, controls, and enclosure strategy
- often sits between site utilities and internal compute environments as the deployable facility unit
- interacts heavily with logistics, site selection, EPC, commissioning, and operations planning
- may be used for AI expansion, edge deployments, temporary capacity, phased campus growth, or specialized environments

## What to compare

- deployment speed, factory integration depth, and site-work requirements
- density support, thermal architecture, and electrical flexibility
- expandability, repeatability, and support for phased growth
- transport constraints, installation complexity, and regional deployment support
- suitability for enterprise, edge, AI, bitcoin, colo, or hybrid retrofit use cases

## Typical buyer questions

- Is modular the best fit versus a traditional build, retrofit, or colo path?
- What is actually delivered as a packaged system versus left to site contractors?
- What density can the design support now and after future upgrades?
- How much civil, utility, and permitting work still has to happen on-site?
- How quickly can usable capacity come online once the project is approved?

## Decision scenarios

Modular makes the most sense when the buyer is trying to reduce schedule risk, standardize repeatable capacity, or deploy into places where traditional construction is too slow or too exposed. It should be evaluated as a full delivery model, not simply a container or enclosure choice.

- Fast AI capacity launch: compare factory integration depth, power density, cooling architecture, controls, and commissioning support before assuming modular is faster end-to-end.
- Phased campus growth: evaluate whether modules, electrical rooms, cooling skids, and monitoring can repeat across phases without redesigning every block.
- Edge or remote deployment: weigh transport constraints, site prep, service access, security, network connectivity, and local permitting before selecting a packaged system.
- Temporary or bridge capacity: prioritize vendors that can support relocation, rental-like deployment models, rapid energization, and clear handoff into permanent infrastructure.

## Deployment signals

- Demand timing is ahead of traditional construction, but power, permits, or site work may still limit the real schedule.
- The buyer wants repeatable capacity blocks across multiple sites rather than a one-off bespoke facility.
- Density, cooling, and controls need to be designed together inside the packaged unit.
- Logistics, crane access, transport dimensions, or field commissioning are emerging as project risks.
