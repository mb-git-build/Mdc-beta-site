# Liquid Cooling Providers

This category covers vendors relevant to liquid cooling and advanced thermal management for dense AI and HPC workloads. It includes direct-to-chip approaches, coolant distribution, secondary-loop design, supporting integration layers, and the supplier ecosystem around high-density thermal control.

As rack density climbs, thermal design stops being a side consideration and becomes one of the main constraints in infrastructure planning. Liquid cooling is often where operators move when air cooling alone starts to strain under the realities of modern AI clusters.

## Why this category matters

- liquid cooling can unlock higher rack densities than conventional air approaches can comfortably support
- cooling architecture directly influences power efficiency, floor planning, service models, and operational complexity
- buyers need to distinguish between marketing claims and what a vendor can actually support in production environments
- the cooling decision affects adjacent infrastructure, including HVAC, CDU design, monitoring, water strategy, and maintenance workflows

## Where it fits in a modular deployment

- often sits between dense compute loads and the broader thermal rejection strategy
- can be integrated inside packaged modular systems or introduced as part of targeted retrofits
- interacts with rear-door cooling, direct-to-chip systems, immersion approaches, and facility monitoring layers
- becomes central when modular builds are designed around AI training, HPC, or unusually high heat density

## What to compare

- direct-to-chip versus broader liquid-loop architecture and fit for the target density
- retrofit friendliness versus greenfield optimization
- operational complexity, maintenance needs, and service requirements
- integration with existing plant, monitoring, and thermal rejection systems
- vendor experience with production AI or HPC environments rather than only lab-scale deployments

## Typical buyer questions

- At what density does liquid cooling become worth serious consideration?
- Is this a retrofit fit, a modular new-build fit, or both?
- What supporting infrastructure changes come with the cooling approach?
- How much operational change will the facilities team need to absorb?
- Which vendors align with our deployment model, density target, and risk tolerance?

## Decision scenarios

Liquid cooling research should start from the workload and operating model, not just the cooling product. The right path changes depending on whether the buyer is retrofitting a live room, launching modular AI capacity, or designing a new high-density environment from scratch.

- GPU cluster retrofit: compare rack power density, rear-door or direct-to-chip options, CDU placement, controls integration, and whether existing heat rejection can absorb the load.
- Modular AI deployment: evaluate packaged cooling loops, factory integration, leak detection, commissioning support, and how the module connects to site-level thermal rejection.
- Campus-scale density upgrade: map liquid loops, electrical upgrades, water strategy, service access, monitoring, and redundancy before selecting a vendor family.
- Operations-risk reduction: prioritize vendors with field service depth, clear maintenance procedures, spares strategy, and production references in comparable density ranges.

## Deployment signals

- Air cooling can no longer handle the projected rack load without unacceptable fan energy, floor planning, or reliability tradeoffs.
- GPU roadmap shifted from moderate density to high-density clusters, changing both electrical and cooling assumptions.
- Existing HVAC can reject heat, but rack-level capture and transport need a different architecture.
- Facilities teams need better telemetry, leak response, and service workflow clarity before accepting liquid systems.
