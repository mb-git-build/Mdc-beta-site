## 3. Three Launch Tracks

### 3.1 Graph Credibility
The public graph must feel credible, bounded, and internally coherent.

Launch intent:
- users can browse categories and companies without obvious taxonomy slop
- top branches feel intentionally curated rather than merely accumulated
- company placement is defensible
- adjacency and role clarity are visible enough to justify public trust

### 3.2 Executive Experience
The institution must make immediate sense to a serious outsider.

Launch intent:
- a first-time visitor can understand what the institution is
- core routes each have a distinct, non-overlapping job
- no route feels like a leftover concept or a confused funnel
- the public experience feels disciplined, not unfinished

### 3.3 Launch Operations
The institution must be operable under public traffic and scrutiny.

Launch intent:
- deployment can be executed safely
- rollback can be executed quickly
- moderation and submission handling are understood
- analytics and indexing are intentional
- trust, policy, and operational surfaces exist before public exposure

## 4. P0 / P1 / P2 Scope

### P0 — required before public beta
- credibility hardening of the launch-critical branches
- executive-route clarification for core public routes
- trust and policy layer
- analytics measurement plan
- Search Console/indexing readiness
- sitemap and robots verification
- deployment runbook and rollback procedure
- moderation workflow and production verification checklist
- repository artifact hygiene sufficient for launch operations

### P1 — strongly preferred before public beta
- targeted deepening of launch-critical branches where credibility gaps remain
- route differentiation improvements across categories/directory/vendors/compare
- better visible evidence signals and scanability on key public routes
- selective discovery improvements that make the existing graph easier to use
- targeted cleanup of public-route confusion or thin-page embarrassment risks

### P2 — explicitly deferred until after launch
- broad or open-ended ecosystem expansion
- non-launch-critical ontology expansion
- cosmetic novelty or visual reinvention
- large guide-library expansion
- recommendation behavior or buyer-intake drift
- speculative expansion into adjacent branches without direct launch value
- advanced graph intelligence layers not required for beta trust

### Scope clarifications
- **Targeted deepening of 3–5 launch-critical branches is P0/P1.**
- **Broad or open-ended ecosystem expansion is P2 and deferred.**
- **Cosmetic novelty, large guide expansion, recommendation behavior, and non-launch-critical ontology expansion are deferred.**

## 5. Launch-Critical Branches

These are the backbone lanes that must pass credibility review before launch.

### 5.1 Power & Electrical Infrastructure
**Why it matters**
- foundational to the compute infrastructure story
- one of the clearest tests of whether the directory understands the actual stack

**Launch acceptance criteria**
- category/subcategory boundaries are coherent
- major role classes are represented clearly
- company placements are defensible
- adjacent systems are visible (backup power, distribution, controls, deployment context)
- no obviously embarrassing misclassification in public-facing core entries

### 5.2 Cooling / Liquid Cooling / Thermal Pathways
**Why it matters**
- highly scrutinized by technically literate users
- a proving ground for relationship clarity and adjacency logic

**Launch acceptance criteria**
- liquid cooling, thermal rejection, and related cooling paths are distinguishable
- company role clarity is visible
- adjacent infrastructure links are useful
- no obvious flattening of distinct thermal categories into one vague blob
- the branch feels technically serious, not trend-chasing

### 5.3 Modular / Prefab / Containerized Infrastructure
**Why it matters**
- closest to the institution’s brand anchor
- central to credibility of the domain name itself

**Launch acceptance criteria**
- modular/prefab/containerized distinctions are clear enough for beta
- branch expresses real deployment context, not just branded vendor grouping
- category pages and company pages reinforce the market-map thesis
- this branch can withstand founder and outsider scrutiny as the anchor lane

### 5.4 AI Colocation & GPU Hosting
**Why it matters**
- likely one of the most commercially legible branches for early visitors
- bridges infrastructure and operational demand-side interest

**Launch acceptance criteria**
- the branch distinguishes hosting/colo roles with enough clarity for public beta
- company inclusion and categorization feel defensible
- public pages do not drift into matchmaking or recommendation behavior
- the branch supports exploration without implying endorsement

### 5.5 Monitoring / Controls / DCIM / Operations
**Why it matters**
- proves the institution understands that the stack is not only hardware
- improves realism and seriousness of the graph

**Launch acceptance criteria**
- monitoring, controls, and operations layers are visible as real infrastructure functions
- category scope is not hand-wavy
- representative companies are placed credibly
- branch clearly links to adjacent deployment and infrastructure layers

## 6. Core Executive Routes

Every route below must have a distinct responsibility and measurable acceptance criteria.

### `/`
**Responsibility**
- establish what the institution is
- frame the directory as a category-first market map
- orient serious first-time visitors toward the right entry points

**Acceptance criteria**
- clear statement of purpose within one screen
- primary navigation choice is obvious
- does not overpromise beyond current institutional maturity
- no buyer-funnel or recommendation-engine framing dominates the page

### `/categories`
**Responsibility**
- present the category graph as the primary browse surface
- communicate breadth without chaos

**Acceptance criteria**
- category structure is scan-friendly
- branch groupings feel intentional
- route is useful even for a first-time visitor who does not know vendor names
- no visible taxonomy confusion in the top-level market map

### `/directory`
**Responsibility**
- serve as the practical market-map browse layer
- help visitors move from broad segment to narrower discovery path

**Acceptance criteria**
- route is distinct from `/categories`
- entry paths feel useful, not duplicative
- supports progression from segment to company exploration
- no conceptual overlap that makes the route feel unnecessary

### `/vendors`
**Responsibility**
- provide the company index and company discovery layer
- allow users to browse companies without undermining category-first doctrine

**Acceptance criteria**
- company index is usable and credible
- browsing companies does not replace the category-first mental model
- route does not look like a random list dump
- company positioning and scanability feel intentional

### `/methodology`
**Responsibility**
- explain how the institution thinks
- establish the editorial and structural logic behind the graph

**Acceptance criteria**
- clearly explains categorization intent
- makes the graph feel more trustworthy
- avoids sounding vague, inflated, or unfinished
- supports public trust rather than internal note-taking

### `/about`
**Responsibility**
- explain why the institution exists and what it covers
- provide a credible institutional identity

**Acceptance criteria**
- concise and externally legible
- consistent with the directory-first doctrine
- does not drift into founder mythology or startup pitch language
- helps a skeptical outsider understand the mission without oversell

### Launch decision: `/compare`
**Decision:** keep only if it supports the authoritative-directory doctrine with a clear, non-funnel role.

**Launch acceptance criteria**
- must clearly help users understand category or supplier comparison in a directory context
- must not feel like disguised buyer intake or sales conversion scaffolding
- if it cannot meet that bar by executive-route freeze, demote or hide it from public launch navigation

### Launch decision: `/get-matched`
**Decision:** public route is presumptively in conflict with authoritative-directory doctrine.

**Launch rule**
- it should not remain public in a way that suggests lead-matching is a core product promise
- if retained at all before launch, it must be clearly demoted, reframed, or removed from public navigation
- if route purpose cannot be reconciled with doctrine, it should not remain public at launch

**Non-negotiable rule**
- no route should remain public if its purpose conflicts with the authoritative-directory doctrine

## 16. Working Principle for Every Session Until Launch

Before starting major work, ask:
1. Does this materially improve trust before August 31?
2. Does this strengthen graph credibility, executive experience, or launch operations?
3. If not, why is it not being deferred?

If the answer is weak, the work should stop.
