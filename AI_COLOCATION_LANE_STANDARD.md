# AI_COLOCATION_LANE_STANDARD.md

## 1. Purpose

This document defines the inclusion and exclusion standard for the **AI Colocation & GPU Hosting** backbone lane.

Canonical lane slug:
- `ai-colocation-gpu-hosting`

Current subordinate launch-use slug:
- `managed-ai-colocation`

This standard exists to protect launch credibility before August 31, 2026.

It is a doctrine document, not an expansion invitation.

Its job is to keep the lane:
- precise
- defensible
- buyer-legible
- internally consistent
- aligned with the authoritative-directory doctrine

This lane is part of the launch-critical graph. It must therefore favor **precision over density**.

---

## 2. Governing Doctrine

This lane standard inherits the institution’s controlling doctrine:
- authoritative directory first
- evidence-first graph discipline
- no recommendation-engine behavior
- no buyer-funnel drift
- no broad expansion that weakens launch coherence
- no public ambiguity about what a company actually does

This means:
- a company does **not** belong in the lane merely because it is relevant to AI infrastructure
- a company does **not** belong merely because it sells to data centers
- a company does **not** belong merely because hosted GPU workloads may depend on it
- adjacency must be expressed through relationships and neighboring categories, not by bloating lane membership

The lane should help a serious buyer answer:
- Who can actually provide AI colocation, GPU hosting, or managed accelerator capacity?
- Which operators are real capacity pathways versus surrounding suppliers?
- What kind of operator is each company?

---

## 3. Lane Definition

### 3.1 What the lane is

The **AI Colocation & GPU Hosting** lane exists to capture organizations that provide a real capacity pathway for buyers seeking deployed accelerated compute without building and owning the full facility stack themselves.

This includes organizations that directly offer one or more of the following:
- AI-ready colocation environments
- managed colocation for GPU-dense deployments
- hosted GPU infrastructure
- managed accelerator clusters
- AI cloud or hosted capacity that functions as a real deployment alternative to owned infrastructure
- operator-led capacity environments where buyers can place, lease, consume, or contract AI compute infrastructure

### 3.2 What the lane is not

The lane is **not** the general map of everything relevant to AI infrastructure.

It is not for:
- cooling vendors
n- network vendors
- switch or fabric providers
- DCIM or monitoring vendors
- rack, power, or enclosure suppliers
- engineering firms
- integrators unless they also operate capacity directly
- site developers without a real operator-facing capacity offer
- adjacency players whose importance is relational rather than operator-based

A company may be strategically important to this lane and still **not belong inside it**.

---

## 4. What Organizations Belong

An organization belongs in `ai-colocation-gpu-hosting` if it meets the lane’s operator threshold and evidence threshold.

### 4.1 Primary inclusion classes

A company belongs if it is clearly one of these:

#### A. AI colocation operator
An organization that provides physical colocation capacity and explicitly positions itself as relevant to GPU-dense or AI-oriented deployments.

Examples of qualifying behaviors:
- markets AI-ready colocation environments
- highlights high-density rack support for AI/GPU clusters
- offers deployment-ready colocation for accelerator-heavy workloads
- provides enterprise or hyperscale colocation that is explicitly framed as suitable for AI infrastructure

#### B. Managed AI colocation operator
An organization that offers operationally managed AI capacity environments rather than simple neutral-space colocation.

Examples of qualifying behaviors:
- powered and managed GPU-ready environments
- deployment support around hosted AI infrastructure
- capacity contracts that behave more like managed AI infrastructure than plain colo square footage

#### C. GPU hosting / AI cloud operator
An organization that directly provides hosted GPU capacity or AI compute environments as a substitute for self-built infrastructure.

Examples of qualifying behaviors:
- GPU cloud / hosted accelerator platform
- dedicated AI cluster hosting
- managed training/inference infrastructure
- enterprise AI compute capacity sold as an operational service

#### D. Hybrid power-plus-compute operator
An organization that directly operates AI-relevant compute capacity in a nontraditional but real hosting/colocation model, especially when the buyer is acquiring deployed capacity rather than merely equipment.

This class should be used carefully.
It is for actual operators, not conceptually adjacent developers.

## 5. What Organizations Do Not Belong

A company does **not** belong in this lane if it is any of the following without also meeting operator criteria.

### 5.1 Adjacent suppliers
These belong in neighboring categories, not as lane members:
- cooling OEMs
- immersion vendors
- direct-to-chip or rear-door cooling specialists
- networking vendors
- interconnect vendors
- optical/connectivity hardware vendors
- high-density power vendors
- UPS, switchgear, PDU, or busway suppliers
- monitoring / controls / DCIM vendors
- prefabrication suppliers
- component OEMs

### 5.2 Service-layer adjacencies
These may be strategically important but should usually be expressed through relationships, not lane membership:
- cloud interconnection platforms
- carrier-neutral exchange ecosystems
- connectivity marketplaces
- brokerage or orchestration layers

### 5.3 Delivery and advisory firms
These do not belong unless they also operate real capacity:
- EPC firms
- engineering consultants
- data center advisors
- construction managers
- integrators without operator control of compute environments

### 5.4 Infrastructure-enabling companies
These may be critical to the lane but remain adjacent unless they operate capacity directly:
- power developers
- site developers
- land strategy firms
- microgrid specialists
- energy strategy firms

## 6. What Constitutes an Operator

For this lane, an **operator** is an organization that directly controls, offers, or delivers AI-relevant compute capacity or colocation capacity as a real buyer-facing deployment option.

A company should be treated as an operator only if the evidence supports at least one of the following:
- it directly offers colocation capacity for customer deployments
- it directly offers managed AI colocation or AI-ready facility capacity
- it directly offers hosted GPU infrastructure or managed accelerator clusters
- it directly operates AI cloud / hosted compute environments that buyers can contract as a deployment pathway
- it publicly presents itself as the entity responsible for delivering the capacity environment, not merely supporting it

### Operator test
A simple test:

**If a serious buyer asked, “Can I actually get AI deployment capacity from this organization?” the answer should be clearly yes.**

If the answer is only:
- “they supply part of the stack,” or
- “they enable others to host,” or
- “they are important to AI infra,”

then the company is usually **not** a lane member.

---

## 7. What Constitutes an Adjacent Supplier

An **adjacent supplier** is an organization whose products or services materially support AI colocation or GPU hosting, but which does not itself provide the capacity pathway.

Examples:
- a networking company whose gear powers GPU clusters
- a cooling company whose systems enable dense AI hosting
- an interconnection platform linking cloud and colo environments
- a deployment integrator packaging GPU infrastructure
- a monitoring vendor used by AI operators

These companies matter.
They should often appear in:
- adjacent categories
- related company relationships
- often-used-with relationships
- category adjacency language
- narrative comparisons

They should **not** be included in the lane merely to make the lane look denser.

---

## 8. Evidence Required for Inclusion

No company should be included in this lane without evidence that it actually fits the operator standard.

### 8.1 Minimum evidence threshold
A company should generally have at least one strong public signal showing direct operator relevance, preferably more than one.

Acceptable evidence types include:
- company page explicitly offering AI colocation, GPU hosting, hosted GPU clusters, or AI cloud infrastructure
- public product or solutions pages describing customer-accessible AI compute capacity
- evidence of GPU-dense colocation or DGX / accelerator-oriented hosting environments
- operator language around managed clusters, AI infrastructure capacity, or hosted accelerator deployments
- clearly stated market positioning as an AI infrastructure or GPU hosting operator

### 8.2 Weak evidence that is not enough on its own
The following is not enough by itself:
- generic AI mention
- generic “supports AI workloads” phrasing
- generic data center relevance
- being active in colocation without AI-specific or GPU-density relevance
- broad cloud language without clear hosted GPU / AI infra evidence
- reputation alone

### 8.3 Evidence quality preference
Prefer evidence that is:
1. explicit
2. current
3. buyer-facing
4. operator-specific
5. difficult to misread

The goal is not maximum coverage.
The goal is defensible inclusion.

## 9. Borderline Companies

Borderline companies must be handled conservatively.

### 9.1 Borderline rule
If a company could reasonably be read either as:
- an operator
nor
- an adjacent supplier,

then it should default to **hold / review**, not inclusion.

### 9.2 Borderline examples
Borderline companies often include:
- interconnection-heavy operators whose AI relevance is indirect
- energy-linked compute developers whose operator role is still ambiguous
- GPU marketplaces with weak institutional evidence
- cloud providers with some GPU offerings but unclear lane centrality
- integrators with deployment credibility but no direct hosting role

### 9.3 Handling method
For borderline cases:
- do not stretch the lane definition
- do not include for density
- document why the record is borderline
- prefer relationship links or neighboring categories until the operator case is clearer

When in doubt: **hold, don’t over-tag**.

## 10. Relationships vs Over-Tagging

A core rule of this lane is:

**Use relationships to express adjacency. Do not use lane membership to express relevance.**

### 10.1 When to use relationships instead of lane inclusion
Use relationships when a company:
- powers the lane
- cools the lane
- networks the lane
- monitors the lane
- enables deployment in the lane
- is commonly partnered with lane operators
- is often compared during buyer evaluation but is not itself a direct capacity operator

### 10.2 Relationship mechanisms
Appropriate mechanisms include:
- `related_company_slugs`
- `adjacent_category_slugs`
- `often_used_with`
- neighboring category inclusion
- buyer-facing explanation in category pages and vendor pages

### 10.3 Why this matters
This protects:
- graph integrity
- ontology consistency
- buyer clarity
- institutional credibility

If everything relevant gets tagged into the lane, the lane stops meaning anything.

## 11. Inclusion Hierarchy for Launch

Because this is a launch-critical backbone lane, inclusion should follow a hierarchy.

### Tier 1 — clear operator anchors
Keep and prioritize organizations that unmistakably provide:
- colocation capacity
- managed AI colocation
- hosted GPU capacity
- AI cloud / managed accelerator infrastructure

These are the backbone of the lane.

### Tier 2 — credible but narrower operator participants
Include organizations that clearly operate relevant capacity but may be:
- more specialized
- more regional
- more emerging
- more model-specific

These strengthen realism if evidence is adequate.

### Tier 3 — borderline or relationally important companies
Do not include automatically.
These should usually remain in hold or adjacency form unless evidence is unusually strong.

## 12. Buyer-Clarity Standard

Every company included in this lane should help a serious buyer understand one of the real deployment choices available before launch.

The lane should be legible to a buyer asking:
- Should I use colocation?
- Should I use managed AI capacity?
- Should I contract hosted GPU infrastructure?
- Which operators are infrastructure pathways versus surrounding vendors?

If a company makes that answer muddier instead of clearer, it should probably not be in the lane.

Buyer clarity matters more than ecosystem completeness.

## 13. Precision Over Density Rule

This lane explicitly favors **precision over density**.

That means:
- fewer correct members are better than more ambiguous ones
- a lane with 14 strong operators is better than a lane with 21 mixed-role entries
- adjacency inflation is a launch risk, not a launch win

The institution should prefer a smaller, sharper branch that can withstand scrutiny over a broader branch that collapses under basic questioning.

## 15. Practical Review Checklist

For every candidate in `ai-colocation-gpu-hosting`, ask:

1. Does this company directly provide customer-accessible AI colocation, GPU hosting, or managed accelerator capacity?
2. Is the operator role explicit in public evidence?
3. Would a buyer reasonably treat this organization as a deployment pathway, not merely a supplier?
4. Is inclusion increasing clarity, not just count?
5. Would adjacency be better represented through relationships instead?

If the answers are weak, contradictory, or mostly relational:
- remove from lane consideration
- place in adjacent category/relationship structure
- hold for later review if needed

---

## 16. Standard Outcome Categories

Each reviewed company should end in one of five states:

### Include
Clear operator fit and evidence threshold met.

### Include with caution
Operator fit exists, but evidence or role clarity is narrower than ideal.

### Hold
Potential fit, but not enough clarity to justify launch-critical inclusion.

### Adjacent only
Important to the lane, but should be expressed through relationships or neighboring categories rather than direct membership.

### Exclude
Does not meet operator standard.
